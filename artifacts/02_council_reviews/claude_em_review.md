# Council Review — Electromagnetic Design & Motor Topology
**Seat:** EM design / motor topology
**Reviewer:** Claude (grand council seat)
**Subject:** CBJG / RSD benchtop BLDC engine, 300–400 W, $10k budget, Facebook Marketplace sourcing
**Verdict up front:** The RSD math foundations doc is fine as an estimator/scheduler spec, but its EM section is thin, generic, and in a couple of places just wrong for the topology it will actually be bolted to. The motor choice is not yet made in the document — it needs to be, explicitly, before any BOM makes sense. This review makes that pick and stress-tests it.

---

## 1. Topology pick: surface-PM outrunner vs. IPM

**Pick: surface-PM (SPM) outrunner.** Not IPM. Here's why, bluntly.

IPM buys you two things: (a) reluctance torque via \((L_d-L_q)\,i_d i_q\) for extra torque-per-amp and flux-weakening range, and (b) mechanical robustness for high-speed / high-centrifugal-load automotive duty. Neither matters here:

- At 300–400 W and ~3000 rpm on a bench, you are nowhere near needing flux-weakening constant-power-speed-range. There's no "highway cruise" regime to optimize for.
- Realizing the saliency ratio \(L_d/L_q\) that makes IPM worth the trouble (\(\gtrsim 1.5\)–2) requires purpose-built lamination geometry with flux barriers — exactly what the NOVATEUR paper is doing in RMxprt/Maxwell 2D from a clean-sheet design. You are not going to find that on Facebook Marketplace used, and you are not going to CNC your own rotor laminations inside a $10k *total* project budget that also has to fund the estimator/scheduler hardware (current sensors, encoder/no-encoder tradeoffs, DSP/MCU, dSPACE-or-equivalent, DC supply). If you self-source rotor punching, welcome to a $30–50k tooling bill just for laminations — see Hanselman Ch. 5 on rotor construction options and why lamination stamping dominates manufacturing cost.
- Every realistic used-marketplace 300–400 W motor (hoverboard hub, e-bike hub/mid-drive, hobby outrunner, HDD/CNC spindle) **is already SPM**. Sourcing an actual IPM motor at this power/price point used is close to impossible outside of scrapped e-bike mid-drives with internal reluctance rotors (rare) or automotive traction motor teardowns (10–50 kW class, wrong power scale, wrong voltage, wrong everything for a benchtop rig).
- The RSD foundations doc *itself* already commits to SPM in §2 ("For a surface-PM rotor (our benchtop build), \(L_d \approx L_q\)") — this section of the doc contradicts §9's citation of the NOVATEUR IPM paper as a design reference. That's an internal inconsistency worth flagging: the doc cites an IPM ANSYS paper as a foundation, then designs for SPM. Pick one. I'm picking SPM, and the reference doc should stop leaning on the IPM paper for anything except the general sizing equation \(T = kD^2L\) and the RMxprt/Maxwell workflow description, both of which are topology-agnostic.

**Outrunner vs. inrunner within SPM:** outrunner. Rotor-cup-outside topology puts the magnets at larger radius for the same stator OD, which per Hanselman Ch. 4 (torque ∝ \(R_{ro}\), Eq. 4.8–4.11) directly buys torque without adding stack length — exactly the lever a benchtop 1 Nm target needs from a small, cheap frame. It's also the native form factor of every hobby/e-bike/hoverboard motor you'll find used, so this isn't a fight worth picking against the marketplace.

## 2. Pole/slot combination

**Pick: 12N14P (12 slots, 14 poles) as primary target; 9N8P acceptable fallback; reject 24N20P for this power class.**

Hanselman Ch. 6 frames this correctly: pole/slot choice is about (a) whether a *balanced 3-phase winding* even exists for the combination (§6.3, the phase-offset condition Eq. 6.3–6.6), and (b) the resulting winding factor \(K_{wn}\) (Eq. 6.13) and cogging behavior, not raw slot count.

- **Integral-slot options (e.g., 4-pole/12-slot, \(N_{spp}=1\))** give winding factor = 1 at the fundamental (all coil back-EMFs in phase, Ch. 6 example) but suffer *additive* cogging torque — Hanselman is explicit that in an integral-slot motor "the cogging torque created by all magnets are in phase with each other, and the net cogging torque is equal to the product of the number of magnet poles and the cogging torque created by one magnet" (§4.12). For a benchtop demo motor whose whole point is showing off a clean HFI/sensorless estimator at low speed, high cogging torque is exactly what you don't want — it pollutes the \(i_q\) signal you're trying to use for zero-speed position estimation.
- **Fractional-slot combinations** (12N14P, 9N8P, 24N20P — all have \(N_{spp} < 1\) or non-integer) get partial cancellation of cogging across poles because "each magnet appears in a different position relative to the stator slots... the net cogging torque is reduced" (§4.12, using the 4-pole/15-slot example as the textbook illustration of the *principle*, even though the exact ratios differ from these hobby/EV combos). This is the real reason drone and e-bike hub motors overwhelmingly use fractional-slot concentrated windings (12N14P, 9N12P, 12N10P families) — not because someone read a hobbyist forum, but because concentrated fractional-slot windings shorten end turns (fewer copper losses, easier hand-winding if you ever rewind a used stator), cut cogging, and still support a valid balanced winding under Hanselman's phase-offset test (§6.3, Eq. 6.6: valid \(K_0\) exists for an integer \(q\) in range).
- **12N14P specifically:** \(N_{spp} = 12/14/3 = 0.286\), single-tooth concentrated winding, very short end turns → low copper loss for a given \(Ni\), which matters because you are copper-constrained on a $10k / marketplace-sourced budget, not iron-constrained. This is also the dominant combo in real gimbal/hobby outrunners and many e-bike hub motors, so it's what you will actually receive when you buy "an outrunner" used — the choice is as much *acceptance of what the market gives you* as it is a design decision.
- **9N8P** is the other extremely common concentrated-winding combo (used in e-bike hub motors, some drone motors) and is an acceptable fallback with similar cogging-cancellation properties; slightly higher torque ripple than 12N14P in some studies but the difference is second-order for a benchtop demo.
- **24N20P** (\(N_{spp}=0.4\)) — reject for *this* application, not because it's invalid (larger drone/e-bike hub motors do use it), but because it pushes pole count and slot count up for a given frame size, which increases electrical frequency at 3000 rpm mechanical (poles/2 × rpm/60 → 20 pole pairs is absurd; verify actual pole count before buying — many "24N20P" hub motors are large-diameter, low-speed direct-drive designs tuned for ~200–400 rpm wheel speeds at 36 V, not 3000 rpm benchtop speeds). If you buy a hoverboard hub motor advertised this way, check its *rated speed*, not just pole/slot count — see risk #3 below.

**What Hanselman does NOT give you directly:** a table mapping "12N14P" style shorthand to explicit winding-factor numbers — the corpus excerpt here only works fully-worked examples for 4-pole/12-slot, 4-pole/15-slot, and 10-pole/12-slot (Figs. 6-8–6-10). The qualitative conclusion (fractional-slot → cogging cancellation, shorter end turns) transfers, but if the estimator's HFI mode depends on torque ripple / cogging amplitude being small (it does — see §6 below), don't take 12N14P's superiority on faith. Get the actual winding factor and cogging torque curve for whatever specific used motor you buy, either from a teardown-and-count of slots/poles run through Hanselman's Eq. 6.2–6.13, or from an RMxprt model built from measured geometry.

## 3. Realistic ballpark parameters

State assumptions first: 350 W outrunner, 12N14P (7 pole pairs), 24–36 V class (typical for e-bike/hoverboard-adjacent hardware — NOT the 3-phase-mains-adjacent 230 V of the NOVATEUR paper, which was sized for a totally different — industrial single-phase-equivalent-230V-bus — application and should not be used as a voltage reference here), target ~1 Nm at 3000 rpm mechanical.

| Parameter | Ballpark | Basis / assumption |
|---|---|---|
| \(R_s\) (phase, line-to-neutral) | 30–90 mΩ | Typical hobby/e-bike outrunner stators wound with 18–22 AWG-equivalent multi-strand litz; scales inversely with turns² for fixed slot fill (Hanselman §4.9, "constancy of \(Ni\)") |
| \(L_d, L_q\) | 30–80 µH, \(L_d \approx L_q\) | SPM ⇒ negligible saliency; low-inductance because of large effective air gap (surface magnets + retention sleeve) and few turns for a low-voltage, high-\(K_v\) design |
| \(\psi_m\) (PM flux linkage) | 3–8 mWb-turns equiv. (i.e., \(K_e\) below) | Derived from \(K_e\), not measured directly — Hanselman's point (§4.10) that \(K_t\)/\(K_e\) are set by \(N\) and are not independently meaningful design targets |
| \(K_t = K_e\) | 0.03–0.08 N·m/A | Hanselman Eq. 4.9: \(K_e = K_t\) numerically in SI units. Consistent with \(K_v\) below via \(K_v[\text{rpm/V}] \approx 9.55/K_e[\text{V·s/rad}]\) |
| \(K_v\) | 120–300 rpm/V | Typical for a 300–400 W outrunner intended to hit 3000 rpm on 24–36 V bus; **this is the actual free design variable** (via turns count \(N\)), not \(R_s\) or the magnets |
| Rated current \(I_{rated}\) (RMS, per phase) | 10–18 A | From \(P \approx 1.5\, V_{ph}I_{ph}\eta\cos\varphi\) at 300–400 W and 24–36 V bus |
| Rated voltage (bus) | 24–36 V DC | Matches e-bike/hoverboard/drone-battery ecosystem you're actually sourcing from; do NOT assume 48 V+ without checking what's on the used board/battery you're pairing it with |
| Base speed | 2500–3500 rpm | Set by \(K_v \times V_{bus}\) minus IR drop; chosen to bracket the 3000 rpm target with margin for the EIG scheduler to move around in |

**The one number the RSD doc should stop treating as free:** \(K_v\) (or equivalently \(N\), the turns count) is the actual knob. Everything else (\(R_s\), \(L_d\), \(\psi_m\)) is downstream of the physical rotor/stator you buy used and the winding job it already has. If you rewind a used stator to hit a target \(K_v\), Hanselman's constancy-of-\(Ni\) result (§4.9, Eq. 4.39) tells you exactly what you're trading: more turns → higher \(K_t\)/\(K_e\), proportionally higher \(R_s\) (∝ \(N^2\) for fixed slot fill), motor constant \(K_m\) unchanged. Don't expect a rewind to buy you more torque-per-amp; it only rescales the voltage/current operating point.

## 4. Rotor D²L target for ~1 Nm @ 3000 rpm

Mechanical power at the target point: \(P = T\omega = 1\,\text{N·m} \times 3000 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} \approx 314\,\text{W}\) — consistent with the 300–400 W class stated in the brief. Good, the numbers hang together.

Using Hanselman's air-gap shear-stress formulation (§4.11, Eq. 4.43): \(T = 2\pi R_{ro}^2 L_{st}\,\sigma\), i.e. \(D^2 L \propto T/\sigma\). Hanselman gives typical shear stress bands: low-cost brushless PM motors \(0.5 < \sigma < 2\) psi, higher-cost \(1.5 < \sigma < 3\) psi, high-performance \(2 < \sigma < 10\) psi, large liquid-cooled \(10 < \sigma < 20\) psi. A used hobby/e-bike outrunner is squarely in the low-to-mid-cost band, call it **\(\sigma \approx 1.0\)–1.5 psi (6.9–10.3 kPa)**.

Solving for \(D^2 L\) at \(T=1\) N·m:

\[
D^2 L = \frac{4T}{2\pi \sigma} = \frac{2T}{\pi\sigma}
\]

| \(\sigma\) (psi) | \(D^2L\) required |
|---|---|
| 0.5 (cheap/worn magnets, big air gap) | ~185 cm³ |
| 1.0 (typical used hobby outrunner) | ~92 cm³ |
| 1.5 (decent NdFeB, tight gap) | ~62 cm³ |
| 2.0 (fresh N42+ magnets, well-built) | ~46 cm³ |

**Target: \(D^2L \approx 60\text{–}90\ \text{cm}^3\)**, i.e. rotor magnet-active diameter/length combos like 63 mm OD × 15–23 mm stack, or 70 mm OD × 9–19 mm stack. This maps directly onto real hardware: a typical 5065/6374-class hobby outrunner or hoverboard 6.5" hub motor stator has almost exactly this active volume. This is a sanity-check number, not a purchase spec — measure the actual candidate motor's magnet OD and stack length and plug into the same formula before buying, especially for worn/demagnetized used magnets where real \(\sigma\) may sag toward the 0.5 psi end.

**Caveat the RSD doc glosses over:** \(T \propto kD^2L\) (its Eq. in §2, restated from NOVATEUR Eq. 1 and Hanselman §1) hides all of the actual design difficulty inside the constant \(k\), which Hanselman decomposes into \(N_m, B_g, Ni\) (Eq. 4.42) — pole count, air-gap flux density, and ampere-turns. Citing \(T \propto D^2L\) as if it's a sizing law on its own, without \(k\), is true but nearly vacuous; it doesn't tell you anything about whether a *specific* used motor will hit 1 Nm until you've estimated \(B_g\) (from magnet grade + gap, itself dependent on the permeance coefficient \(P_c\), Hanselman §4.2, Fig. 4-4) and \(Ni\) (from the winding you've got or plan to put in). The RSD document should either drop the \(kD^2L\) equation or expand it to the shear-stress form with an explicit \(\sigma\) assumption, because as written it reads like it's doing more work than it is.

## 5. Three used-motor SKUs from Facebook Marketplace, with rough prices

1. **SunnySky/Turnigy-class hobby outrunner (e.g., SunnySky X4108S family, 380–690 KV, 24-slot/22-pole)** — [manufacturer spec sheet](https://www.himodel.com/electric/SUNNYSKY_X4108S_480KV_Outrunner_Brushless_Motor_for_Multi-rotor_Aircraft.html) shows 400–450 W continuous rating, ~46 mm OD × 25.5 mm stack, ~110–113 g. Used pricing on Marketplace typically $20–40 for a single unit (new retail is [$70–75](https://www.hobbytech.com.au/products/rc-accessories/motors/sunnysky-x4108s-380kv-out-runner-bl-motor/)). Note: this is a 24N22P combo (\(N_{spp}=0.36\)), not 12N14P, but shares the fractional-slot concentrated-winding cogging-cancellation logic from §2 above. Good electrical fit; weak mechanical fit (no shaft/bearing arrangement suited to a benchtop dyno without an adapter).
2. **Hoverboard hub motor, 6.5" wheel, 36 V, 350 W** — [typical spec](https://www.alibaba.com/product-detail/6-5-inch-Hoverboard-Motor-Wheel_62390736134.html): 350 W rated, 36 V, ~800 rpm rated wheel speed (i.e., this is a *direct-drive, high-pole-count, low-Kv* motor — 15–25 pole pairs is common for hub motors, per the [robokits spec](https://robokits.co.in/e-bike-parts/hub-motor/6.5inch-36v-350w-brushless-bldc-hub-motor-with-tyre-for-e-bike-and-hoverboard) citing 25 pole pairs). **This does NOT hit 3000 rpm on its native winding** — its whole design point is low-speed/high-torque, wrong end of the speed range for the stated 3000 rpm target. Usable only if you accept ~800 rpm base speed and rescale the torque target downward, or if you're willing to treat it as a slow-speed estimator testbed instead. Used Marketplace price: pulled-from-scrapped-hoverboard units run **$10–25 each**, often sold in pairs.
3. **CNC/spindle BLDC motor, 400 W class, 48 V, ER11 collet type** — [VEVOR spec](https://www.vevor.com/spindle-motor-c_10130/cnc-400w-brushless-spindle-motor-speed-controller-mount-600w-psu-us-stock-p_010543973069): 400 W, 48 V DC, rated 12,000 rpm, 530 mN·m rated torque — this is an *inrunner*, high-Kv, low-torque/high-speed unit, essentially the mirror image of the hoverboard hub. At 3000 rpm it would only be delivering a fraction of its rated torque (power roughly constant if voltage-limited, so ~1 Nm at 3000 rpm is plausible if you accept it's running below its 12k rpm sweet spot — verify against its actual torque-speed curve, don't assume linearity). Used/pulled units from decommissioned hobby CNC routers show up on Marketplace for **$25–50**, sometimes bundled with the driver board.

**Honest bottom line on sourcing:** none of these three is a clean drop-in match to "SPM outrunner, 12N14P, 1 Nm @ 3000 rpm, 24–36 V" simultaneously. You will very likely end up compromising on pole/slot count (accept whatever the used unit has and re-derive \(K_v\)/\(K_t\) empirically) rather than shopping by pole/slot spec — Marketplace listings essentially never state slot/pole count, so #1 above is the most realistic buy-and-verify candidate; #2 and #3 are speed-mismatched and should be treated as fallback/for-parts options, not first picks, unless the project's real target speed turns out to be flexible.

## 6. Top 5 EM-design risks and RSD mitigation/exposure

1. **Unknown/undocumented \(R_s, L_d, L_q, \psi_m\) on a used, unlabeled motor.** You will not get a datasheet for a Marketplace motor. *RSD mitigation:* this is exactly what the EKF/parameter-identification loop (RSD-K, §6 of the foundations doc) is for — online \(R_s\) estimation via the CRLB-gated recursive identification, and \(\psi_m\)/\(K_e\) can be bootstrapped from a simple open-circuit back-EMF spin-up test before closing the loop. RSD doesn't eliminate this risk, but it's the correct tool for it — this is the one place where the RSD framing is not oversold.

2. **Demagnetization / thermal degradation of used magnets, silently lowering \(B_g\) and hence \(K_t\), \(\sigma\), and the D²L margin computed in §4.** A motor bought used from a hoverboard or drone crash may have partially demagnetized magnets from a prior thermal event, and you won't know until you measure. *RSD exposure, not mitigation:* the CUSUM/NIS change-point detector (§7 of the foundations doc) will catch a *sudden* demag event as an innovation spike, but a magnet that's already 15% weak *before you ever start the estimator* just looks like a slightly wrong \(\psi_m\) prior — the EKF will happily converge to a biased-but-self-consistent estimate and never flag it as a problem unless you have an independent ground truth (e.g., a bench dyno torque measurement) to compare against. RSD's "auditability" claim (§8) is only as good as having an independent measurement channel to audit against; without one, the EKF can be confidently wrong.

3. **Pole/slot count and rated speed mismatch to the 3000 rpm target (see SKU #2 and #3 above).** Buying blind off Marketplace risks ending up with a motor whose base speed is 800 rpm or 12,000 rpm, not 3000. *RSD relevance: none.* This is a pure EM/mechanical sourcing risk that no amount of estimator sophistication fixes. Flagging because the RSD doc's framing ("a benchtop 200–400W outrunner already delivers ~1 Nm at ~3000 rpm") is stated as if it's a given, when in reality it's a sourcing constraint that needs active verification per motor.

4. **Cogging torque and torque ripple corrupting the HFI zero-speed signal.** The RSD doc's own observability logic (§4) depends on HFI working cleanly below ~10% rated speed. Cogging torque (Hanselman §4.12) and any winding-factor-driven back-EMF harmonics (Ch. 6, Figs. 6-8–6-10) inject torque ripple at multiples of electrical frequency and at the pole/slot LCM frequency, which can alias into the HFI carrier band, especially on a bargain used motor with loose tolerances, worn bearings, or an eccentric air gap (all likely on Marketplace parts). *RSD mitigation:* the observability Gramian rank test (§4, \(\text{cond}(\mathcal{O}) > 10^6\) rejection) is the right instinct, but the doc as written treats observability failure as a binary speed threshold ("below 10% rated speed... rank-deficient"), when in reality cogging-induced ripple can degrade observability *at any speed*, not just near zero. The doc oversimplifies here — see §7 below.

5. **Thermal derating on a motor with no datasheet and no known insulation class.** The RSD thermal model (§6, two-state \(T_{cu}/T_{fe}\) lumped model) needs \(\tau_{cu}, \tau_{cf}, \tau_{fe}, C_{cu}, C_{fe}\) — none of which you know for a used hoverboard hub motor. *RSD exposure:* the doc says the scheduler "refuses to trust \(\hat T_{cu}\) estimates whose CRLB exceeds ±5K" — good instinct, but if the thermal model's *structure* (not just its parameters) is wrong (e.g., you assumed a lumped 2-state model but the actual motor has a much faster-heating rotor magnet path due to a thin air gap and eddy losses in a poorly-laminated used core), CRLB gating protects you from *noisy* estimates, not from *structurally biased* ones. A confidently-wrong thermal model will pass the CRLB gate and still let you cook the windings.

## 7. What the RSD math foundations doc gets wrong or oversimplifies about the motor physics

Being blunt, as requested:

- **§2 states "For a surface-PM rotor... \(L_d \approx L_q\), so the reluctance term vanishes and \(T_e \approx \frac{3}{2}p[\psi_m i_q]\)."** This is correct as a first-order statement but omits that real SPM motors — especially cheap used ones with non-ideal magnet arc/pole embrace and slotting effects — do exhibit measurable saliency (\(L_d \ne L_q\), typically \(L_q > L_d\) by 5–15%) from stator slot leakage and saturation asymmetry, per the general framing in Hanselman Ch. 4 on non-ideal air-gap flux density and Ch. 7 on saturation. The doc's own EKF (§3) carries \(L_d, L_q\) as separate states, which is right — but then the text asserts they're equal, undermining the reason for carrying them separately. Either commit to \(L_d = L_q\) as a modeling assumption (and drop one state) or acknowledge the real saliency and don't call it zero.

- **§2 cites the NOVATEUR IPM paper's \(T = kD^2L\) (their Eq. 1) as if it's topology-agnostic sizing support for the SPM pick.** It is topology-agnostic as a *scaling law*, yes — but the NOVATEUR paper's specific worked numbers (500 W, 230 V, 4-pole, 12-slot, 3000 rpm IPM design, Table I) are for a completely different voltage class and topology than anything sourced from Facebook Marketplace. Citing this paper's reference design as supporting evidence for a 24–36 V SPM outrunner build is a citation-adjacent-to-the-point move, not actual support. The doc should either drop the NOVATEUR citation from the SPM-relevant sections or explicitly say "we borrow only the RMxprt/Maxwell workflow and the general sizing equation, not the specific machine".

- **§4's observability rejection threshold treats "below rated 10% speed with no HFI" as the only observability-loss regime.** As flagged in risk #4 above, this is an oversimplification: observability can also degrade at *any* speed under high torque ripple, at specific speeds where cogging harmonics beat against the electrical frequency (a resonance-like phenomenon), or when \(i_d = i_q = 0\) transiently (light-load coasting) regardless of speed. The Gramian test (Eq. in §4) is the right general tool, but the doc's prose describes only the low-speed failure mode, which will under-cover the actual observability-loss set for a rough, used, high-cogging motor.

- **§5's energy balance treats \(P_{iron}(\omega, B_{gap})\) as a clean function argument without ever giving its form.** For a used motor with unknown lamination steel grade (very likely non-M19/non-M43 grade steel, more likely generic scrap-grade laminations in a $20 hoverboard motor), iron loss will not follow a textbook Steinmetz curve cleanly, and could easily be the dominant loss term at 3000 rpm rather than copper loss — the doc's framing (copper loss spelled out explicitly, iron loss left as an unspecified function) implicitly under-weights iron loss's importance and uncertainty. This matters because the E-branch clipping and EIG scheduling objective (§5) are only as good as the loss model feeding them; an unmodeled iron loss term is exactly the kind of structural model error that risk #5 above warns about.

- **The document never states an assumed magnetic circuit / permeance coefficient (\(P_c\)) for the actual air gap and magnet grade it expects, despite Hanselman's Ch. 2/4 making clear that \(B_g\) — and therefore \(K_t\), \(\sigma\), and the whole D²L sizing story in §2 — is *entirely* set by \(P_c\)-vs-magnet-grade (Fig. 4-4 in the corpus).** Without at least a placeholder \(B_g\) assumption (e.g., "N35-grade NdFeB, 1mm gap, expect \(B_g \approx 0.5\)–0.7 T"), the "1 Nm at 3000 rpm" claim in §2 is asserted, not derived. This review derives it properly in §4 above using shear stress instead — the RSD doc should adopt that framing or something equivalent rather than leaving \(k\) in \(T=kD^2L\) unstated.

- **Minor terminology point:** §2's state vector and dynamics equations are standard dq-frame IPM/SPM equations (correctly transcribed, matching the structure implied by Hanselman Ch. 3's electrical-mechanical relationships), but calling this "from Hanselman Ch. 3–4" is generous — Hanselman's actual Ch. 3–4 development in this corpus builds up the physics from first principles (flux linkage → BLv/BLi laws → \(K_e = K_t\)) rather than starting from the dq-frame state-space form the RSD doc presents. The dq-frame form is standard textbook synchronous machine theory (more Krause/Bose than Hanselman specifically), and citing it as directly from Hanselman Ch. 3–4 slightly overstates the traceability. Not wrong physics, just a slightly loose citation.

## Summary

SPM outrunner, 12N14P primary / 9N8P fallback, is the defensible pick — IPM is a non-starter for this budget and sourcing channel, full stop. The D²L target (~60–90 cm³) and parameter ballparks above are derived from Hanselman's shear-stress and torque-constant framework and are consistent with real Marketplace hardware, but no specific used-motor SKU cleanly satisfies both the pole/slot preference and the 3000 rpm speed target simultaneously — expect to compromise on sourcing and re-derive parameters empirically rather than shop to spec. The RSD estimator/scheduler layer is well-matched to the *parameter uncertainty* risk inherent in used-motor sourcing, but the foundations document currently overstates how directly its equations follow from Hanselman, understates real SPM saliency and iron-loss uncertainty, and asserts the core \(T=kD^2L\) sizing claim without ever pinning down the flux density / permeance coefficient that actually determines whether it's true for a specific candidate motor.
